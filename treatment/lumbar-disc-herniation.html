What Is Lumbar Disc Herniation?

Your spine is made up of 33 bones called vertebrae, cushioned by soft discs that act like shock absorbers. When one of these discs tears and its gel-like center pushes through, it's called a herniated disc — also known as a slipped or ruptured disc.

When this happens in the lower back (lumbar region), it can press on nearby nerves, causing intense pain, numbness, or weakness that often radiates down your leg — a condition known as sciatica.

This condition is more common than you might think. Studies show that up to 2% of adults experience symptomatic lumbar disc herniation each year, with the highest rates among people aged 30–50.

Symptoms & Diagnosis

Recognizing the signs early can help you seek treatment faster — and potentially avoid surgery.

疼

Lower Back Pain

Persistent or acute pain in your lower back, often worsened by movement, coughing, or sneezing. This is typically the first and most common symptom.

⚡

Sciatica

A sharp, burning sensation that radiates from your lower back through your buttocks and down your leg. You may feel it as far as your calf or foot.

🦶

Numbness & Tingling

Reduced sensation or a "pins and needles" feeling in your leg, foot, or toes. Many patients describe it as their leg "falling asleep" frequently.

💪

Muscle Weakness

Difficulty lifting your foot (foot drop) or weakness in your legs that affects walking. This signals that the herniated disc is pressing on motor nerves.

🦵

Leg Pain > Back Pain

In many cases, leg pain (radiculopathy) is more severe than back pain. If your leg pain is worse than your back pain, this is a key indicator.

⚠️

Loss of Bladder Control

In severe cases, cauda equina syndrome — characterized by bladder/bowel incontinence — requires emergency surgery. Seek immediate medical attention.

Treatment Options

Your treatment path depends on the severity of your condition. We guide you through every option — from conservative care to minimally invasive surgery.

Non-Surgical Treatments

Physical Therapy

Targeted exercises and stretches to strengthen your core, improve flexibility, and relieve pressure on the affected nerve. Often the first-line treatment.

Medications

Anti-inflammatory drugs, muscle relaxants, or nerve pain medications (like gabapentin) can help manage pain and reduce inflammation while you heal.

Epidural Steroid Injections

A corticosteroid injection directly into the affected area can provide significant pain relief for weeks or months, allowing you to participate in therapy.

Surgical Treatments

If conservative treatments haven't worked after 6–12 weeks, or if your symptoms are severe, surgery may be recommended. Our partner surgeons specialize in minimally invasive techniques that mean faster recovery for you.

Microdiscectomy

The most common surgery for herniated discs. A small incision removes the portion of the disc pressing on your nerve. Success rate: 85–95%.

Recovery: 2–6 weeks

Endoscopic Discectomy

A tube-mounted camera guides the surgeon through a tiny incision. Less invasive than microdiscectomy, with minimal tissue damage and faster healing.

Recovery: 1–4 weeks

Spinal Fusion

For severe cases or recurrences, two vertebrae are fused together using bone grafts and hardware. This eliminates motion that causes pain.

Recovery: 3–6 months
