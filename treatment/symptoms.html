Common Symptoms of Lumbar Disc Herniation

A lumbar disc herniation occurs when the soft center of a spinal disc pushes through a tear in the tougher exterior casing, pressing on nerves and causing pain. Symptoms vary depending on the severity and location of the herniation.

⇆

Lower Back Pain

Persistent aching in the lower back is the most common symptom. The pain may worsen with movement, coughing, or sneezing and may radiate to the hips and buttocks.

→

Sciatica

Sharp, burning pain that travels from the lower back through the buttock and down the leg following the path of the sciatic nerve. This occurs when the herniated disc presses on the sciatic nerve.

📁

Leg Numbness or Tingling

A "pins and needles" sensation or complete numbness in the leg, foot, or toes. This indicates nerve compression affecting the sensory pathways.

⚔

Muscle Weakness

Weakness in the leg or foot muscles that may cause difficulty lifting the foot (foot drop) or walking on tiptoes. This signals nerve involvement affecting motor function.

💪

Pain Worsened by Sitting

Standing or walking may feel more comfortable than sitting, as sitting places additional pressure on the lumbar discs. Relief is often found by changing positions frequently.

🧠

Loss of Bladder or Bowel Control

In rare but serious cases, cauda equina syndrome may occur, causing loss of bladder or bowel control. This requires immediate medical attention.

When to See a Doctor

While mild disc herniation symptoms may improve with conservative care within a few weeks, certain signs indicate you should consult a medical professional promptly:

  • Pain lasting more than a few weeks without improvement
  • Pain severe enough to interfere with daily activities or sleep
  • Progressive leg weakness or difficulty walking
  • Numbness in the groin, buttocks, or inner thigh (saddle anesthesia)
  • Loss of bladder or bowel control
  • Pain following a significant injury to the back
  • Unexplained fever accompanied by back pain

Important: If you experience sudden loss of bladder or bowel control or severe leg weakness, seek emergency medical care immediately.

How Lumbar Disc Herniation Is Diagnosed

An accurate diagnosis is essential for effective treatment. Your doctor will typically use a combination of physical examination and imaging studies.

Physical Examination

Your doctor will assess your medical history, examine your back for tenderness, and test:

  • Range of motion and flexibility
  • Muscle strength and reflexes
  • Pain response to specific movements
  • Sensation in the legs and feet

MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging)

MRI is the gold standard for diagnosing disc herniation. It uses magnetic fields and radio waves to create detailed images of the spine, showing:

  • Soft tissue structures including discs and nerves
  • Location and size of the herniation
  • Degree of nerve root compression
  • Condition of other spinal structures

MRI does not use radiation and is non-invasive, making it safe for most patients.

CT (Computed Tomography)

CT scans may be used when MRI is not available or suitable. They provide detailed cross-sectional images of the spine and can reveal:

  • Bone structures and spinal alignment
  • Disc herniation and bone spurs
  • Spinal canal narrowing (stenosis)

CT scans involve radiation exposure but are quick and widely available.

Additional Tests

In some cases, your doctor may recommend:

  • X-rays to rule out other causes of pain such as fractures or infections
  • EMG (Electromyography) to assess nerve function and identify compression location
  • Myelogram to evaluate spinal cord and nerve root compression
