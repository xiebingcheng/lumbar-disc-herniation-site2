Medical Questions

Lumbar disc herniation occurs when the soft center of a spinal disc (the nucleus pulposus) pushes through a tear in the tougher outer ring (annulus fibrosus). This can press on nearby nerves, causing pain, numbness, or weakness in the lower back, buttocks, and legs. It most commonly affects people between ages 30-50 and is often caused by age-related disc degeneration, physical strain, or improper lifting.
Common symptoms include severe lower back pain, pain radiating down one or both legs (sciatica), numbness or tingling in the legs or feet, muscle weakness, and difficulty standing or walking. Symptoms may worsen with certain movements like bending, lifting, or sitting. In severe cases, bowel or bladder dysfunction may occur, which requires immediate medical attention. Yes, many cases improve with conservative treatment. Approximately 80-90% of patients find relief within 6-12 weeks through rest, physical therapy, anti-inflammatory medications, and targeted exercises. However, if pain is severe, persists beyond 6 weeks, or causes significant weakness or mobility issues, surgical intervention may be recommended. Your doctor will advise based on your specific condition and response to conservative care. Explore treatment options.
The main surgical options include: 1) Microdiscectomy - removal of herniated disc material pressing on nerves using a small incision with microscope assistance; 2) Endoscopic discectomy - minimally invasive technique using a tiny camera and instruments through a tube; 3) Percutaneous laser disc decompression - laser reduction of disc material; 4) Spinal fusion - joining vertebrae for instability; and 5) Artificial disc replacement - removing and replacing the damaged disc with an artificial one. View all treatment options.
Microdiscectomy is the most common surgery for lumbar disc herniation. During the procedure, the surgeon makes a small incision (1-2 inches) in the lower back, gently moves aside muscles, and removes the portion of the disc pressing on the nerve. The procedure takes 1-2 hours, and most patients go home the same day or after one overnight stay. Recovery typically involves 2-4 weeks of restricted activity followed by physical therapy. Learn more about microdiscectomy.
Recovery varies by procedure type. For microdiscectomy: light activities resume in 2-4 weeks, full recovery in 6-8 weeks. For endoscopic discectomy: return to light work in 1-2 weeks, full recovery in 4-6 weeks. For spinal fusion: initial healing takes 6-12 weeks, with full fusion occurring over 6-12 months. Most patients can fly home 7-14 days after surgery, depending on their procedure and how they feel. View detailed recovery timeline.
Microdiscectomy has an excellent success rate of 85-95%, with most patients experiencing significant pain relief shortly after surgery. Endoscopic discectomy success rates are similar at 85-92%. Spinal fusion success rates range from 70-85% depending on the underlying condition being treated. Your surgeon will discuss expected outcomes based on your specific diagnosis and health status.
Surgery is typically considered when: 1) Conservative treatments have failed after 6-12 weeks; 2) You have severe, disabling pain that limits daily activities; 3) You experience progressive muscle weakness or numbness; 4) You have difficulty standing or walking; 5) Imaging shows a large disc herniation pressing on nerves. Your surgeon will review your MRI results, symptoms, and physical examination to determine if surgery is appropriate for your case.
